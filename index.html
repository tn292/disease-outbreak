DISEASE OUTBREAK:

Asthma Analysis

In the pursuit of advancing public health, general safety, and medical preparedness, the study of infectious diseases has grown to become a critical area of focus for researchers and health professionals worldwide. The complex nature of disease spread, influenced by an array of factors, including but not limited to, socioeconomic status, environment, sex, and age, demands a comprehensive approach to understand and hopefully mitigate its impacts. The Disease Outbreak project aims to delve deep into the patterns of disease outbreaks, predict future trends, and evaluate the effectiveness of various intervention measures.

Asthma, a prevalent respiratory condition familiar to many, is the specific focus of our research. This decision stems from the widespread impact of asthma and the variability in its prevalence across different populations and geographies. The primary goal of this project is to clarify how asthma affects diverse groups and regions over time. By meticulously analyzing prevalence rates, we aim to uncover underlying trends and patterns, such as seasonal variations and geographical disparities. These analyses will help us identify the populations most at-risk, as well as regions that are disproportionately affected.

Moreover, we are also interested in evaluating the efficacy of public health measures, such as air quality regulations and asthma education programs, to ascertain their role in reducing asthma incidence. Employing advanced predictive modeling techniques, our project seeks to enhance our ability to predict asthma prevalence with some precision.

Through this rigorous research and analysis, we hope to provide valuable insights into asthma prevalence in the United States.

Preliminary Research

Asthma is a chronic disease impacting respiratory airways in the lungs: inflammation causes the airways to narrow, making breathing difficult, and causing coughing, wheezing, and shortness of breath. 8.2% of Americans have asthma, and approximately 300 million people worldwide are asthmatic. There is no cure for asthma, only ways to manage the symptoms to live a normal life.

Scientists are still seeking the true cause of asthma, but many factors are associated with its prevalence. Asthma presumably has a genetic factor, as an individual with an asthmatic parent is three to six times more likely to develop asthma than an individual without one. Air pollution and exposure to smog may raise risk for asthma as well, which has historically led people to believe that those who live in urban areas incur a higher risk of developing asthma.

Additionally, specific groups of people are disproportionately affected by asthma. Low-income and senior adults have the highest asthma rates, deaths, and hospitalizations. Black, Hispanic, and Native Americans are affected by asthma more than other races. People who are overweight and obese are at a greater risk for asthma. Some additional risk factors include smoking (or having a mother who smoked during pregnancy), viral respiratory infections, allergies, and many more. There is even occupational asthma, which is triggered by irritants in the workplace such as vapors, fumes, and dust.

Data Collection and Cleaning

The majority of our visualizations incorporated data from the CDC Data Explorer tool, which allows one to directly download U.S state-based data in a CSV format. CSVs were downloaded according to the measure being studied, which ranged subjects such as the distribution of the asthmatic population of male adults, to presence of formaldehyde in the air geographically. After being collected, the CSVs were read into RStudio, and appropriate R packages were installed. Once all datasets were imported into an RMarkdown file, the variable values needed to be subsetted in order to remove trailing percentage signs, and subsequently converted from an alphanumeric string to a numerical factor. After this processing, the datasets were able to flexibly be plotted in various visualizations, utilizing the pre-installed packages.
