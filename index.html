Asthma Analysis

In the pursuit of advancing public health, general safety, and medical preparedness, the study of diseases has grown to become a critical area of focus for researchers and health professionals worldwide. The complex nature of disease spread, influenced by an array of factors including but not limited to socioeconomic status, environment, sex, and age, demands a comprehensive approach to understand and hopefully mitigate its impacts. The Disease Outbreak project aims to delve into the patterns of disease outbreaks, investigate the reasons for these trends, and predict possible outcomes using this data.

Asthma, a prevalent respiratory condition familiar to many, is the specific focus of our research. This decision stems from the widespread impact of asthma and the variability in its prevalence across different populations and geographies. The primary goal of this project is to investigate how asthma affects diverse groups and regions over time. By analyzing prevalence rates, we aim to uncover underlying trends and patterns, such as seasonal variations and geographical disparities. These analyses will help us identify the populations most at risk, as well as regions that are disproportionately affected. Employing advanced predictive modeling techniques, our project seeks to enhance our ability to predict asthma prevalence with some precision. Through this rigorous research and analysis, we hope to provide valuable insights into asthma prevalence in the United States.

Preliminary Research

Asthma is a chronic disease impacting respiratory airways in the lungs: inflammation causes the airways to narrow, making breathing difficult, and subsequently induces coughing, wheezing, and shortness of breath. Approximately 7.7% of Americans have asthma according to national data from 2021, equating to roughly 25 million people in the United States (CDC, 2023); additionally, approximately 300 million people worldwide are asthmatic as well. (Braman, 2006) While studies have not pinpointed a concrete cure for asthma, there are still ways to manage the systems to live a normal life.

Scientists are still seeking the true cause of asthma, but many factors are linked to its distribution. Asthma presumably has a genetic factor, as an individual with an asthmatic parent is three to six times more likely to develop asthma than an individual without one. (American Lung Association, 2023) Air pollution and exposure to smog may raise the risk of inducing asthma as well, which has historically led people to believe that those who live in urban areas incur a higher risk of developing asthma.

Additionally, specific groups of people are disproportionately affected by asthma. Low-income and senior adults have the highest asthma rates, deaths, and hospitalizations. People in the overweight and obese weight categories are at a greater risk of contracting asthma. Some additional risk factors include smoking (or having a mother who smoked during pregnancy), viral respiratory infections, and allergies. There is even a subset of the disease known as occupational asthma, which is specifically triggered by irritants in the workplace such as vapors, fumes, and dust.

Along with factors that may lead to a larger incidence of asthma, scientists have also examined comorbidities of asthma. Comorbidity is the simultaneous presence of one specific disease with another, which can potentially alter the symptoms or signs of both diseases. One such example of comorbidity with asthma is Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD), which yields many similar symptoms to asthma, including wheezing, chronic coughing, and shortness of breath. This disease affects 11.2 million people in America (American Lung Association, 2023). The two diseases have shown such overlap, and great difficulty differentiating between the two has become so prevalent, that a new diagnosis known as Asthma-COPD Overlap Syndrome (ACOS) has emerged (American Lung Association, 2023). Individuals with ACOS have been shown to exhibit more severe and frequent symptoms than those with asthma or COPD alone. Another comorbidity worth analyzing is obesity, which affects 39.6% of American adults. Obesity is associated with low-grade inflammation (including of the airways), along with an increased amount of adipose tissue, which alters the amount of inflammation hormones cause in the body.

Data Collection and Cleaning

The majority of our visualizations incorporated data from the CDC Data Explorer tool, which allows one to directly download U.S state-based data in a CSV format. CSVs were downloaded according to the measure being studied, which ranged from subjects such as the distribution of the asthmatic population of male adults, to presence of formaldehyde in the air geographically. After being collected, the CSVs were read into RStudio, and appropriate R packages were installed, foremostly ggplot2, tidyverse, and USMAP. Once all datasets were imported into an RMarkdown file, the variable values needed to be subsetted in order to remove trailing percentage signs, and subsequently converted from an alphanumeric string to a numerical factor. After this processing, the datasets were able to flexibly be plotted in various visualizations, utilizing the pre-installed packages.

Other visualizations utilized CSV files from the CDC, WHO and NIH and then imported the data using Python's pandas library. We read the CSV files into DataFrames—a versatile data structure that allows for easy manipulation of the data. We then were able to address issues such as missing values, incorrect data types, and duplicate entries using DataFrame methods.

Once the data was cleaned and processed, we visualized it using Folium, a Python library that aids in creating interactive maps using the Leaflet.js library. To map our datasets with Folium, the DataFrame we acquired was iterated over to extract geographic coordinates and other information, which are then used to add markers to a Folium map.

Finally, the following data collection, cleaning, and tools were used when analyzing asthma prevalence in relation to socioeconomic indicators for New York State (NYS). Data was collected from the following sources:

  • All asthma prevalence data at the NYS county-level was sourced from the New York State Asthma Dashboard. This data was already partially compiled and focused on indicators of asthma prevalence like emergency department visits, hospital discharge data, deaths/death rates, and Medicaid managed care population statistics.
  • Out of these indicators of asthma prevalence, one was initially chosen: general adults' current asthma prevalence. The data for this indicator was itself based on the following survey question: “Do you still have asthma?” with the possible responses being "Yes, No, Don’t know, Refused" from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention’s Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS). The BRFSS data comes from annual statewide telephone and cellular surveys conducted by the CDC.
  • The most recent data on socioeconomic indicators (such as personal income per capita, unemployment rates, and high school dropout rates) by county in NYS was obtained from the Rockefeller Institute of Government.

Afterwards, the data cleaning process mainly involved ensuring consistent data formats for analysis. Pandas was again utilized to read and clean the data, which came in CSV and Excel file formats. Techniques like renaming columns, stripping extraneous spaces, and filtering for specific years (e.g., 2017 and 2018) or for the most recent-year data (2021) was used to prepare the datasets for analysis.

As for visualizations, Seaborn and Plotly Express were utilized; the former was used for static visualizations like the scatterplots whereas the latter was utilized to create the interactive choropleth maps (geographic visualizations which are similar to heat maps) that are delineated by county.

Socioeconomic disparities, such as poverty and limited access to healthcare, are known to exacerbate asthma prevalence, particularly in vulnerable populations. These factors, along with others, underscore the urgent need for comprehensive strategies to address the impacts of wildfires, improve air quality, and reduce socioeconomic inequities to mitigate asthma prevalence in the Pacific Northwest.

Much of the existing literature suggests that rhinovirus, or the common cold, is strongly related to a higher incidence of asthmatic people. Rhinovirus causes inflammation of bronchial epithelial cells, which restricts airflow, exacerbating already restricted airways existing in asthmatic individuals (Khan M. A., 2022). Since rhinovirus has its highest rate of incidence during the winter and early spring months, this may be linked with worsened symptoms for asthmatic individuals. Similarly, during early spring months, pollen counts in many regions of the U.S. are at an all-year high, leading to worsened symptoms of seasonal allergies (such as rhinitis). Since this can irritate the airways, it can further explain exacerbation of those already dealing with asthmatic symptoms.

Asthma prevalence refers to the proportion of the population affected by asthma at a given point in time. According to the CDC, asthma prevalence is measured using data from the National Health Interview Survey (NHIS), where respondents self-report whether they have ever been told by a doctor, nurse, or other health professional that they have asthma, and whether they still have asthma. The current asthma prevalence percentage represents the proportion of individuals who answered "Yes" to both questions. This measure provides an estimate of the burden of asthma on the population and helps public health authorities understand the scope of the problem and allocate resources accordingly.

Asthma Across Gender in the USA

Now, let's explore the distribution of asthma across different demographics, such as gender.

Strikingly, the visualization showing the prevalence of asthmatic women is shaded much darker than the visualization showing the prevalence of asthmatic men. Indeed, women are much more likely to have asthma in their adult years than the male population.

On the contrary, our visuals show the opposite story when it comes to adolescents and children. Male youths have approximately 1.5x higher rates of asthma than female youths.

This gender difference is caused in part by the effects of sex hormones on lung cells, as testosterone hinders an immune cell linked to asthma symptoms, such as inflammation and mucus production in the lungs. Testosterone also has a positive effect on the immune system – it diminishes the presence of a type of immune cell called innate lymphoid cells (ILC), so the immune system becomes less reactive to factors that would normally trigger asthma symptoms. On the contrary, estrogen acts on the immune system to increase the severity of asthma symptoms, and acts indirectly to damage lung mechanics and increase inflammation.

After puberty, women are twice as likely to have asthma as men, and the pattern continues until women hit menopause, and then the asthma rates in women start to decline. In fact, after puberty, asthma becomes about 40 percent more prevalent in women, and women older than 15 are 2.3 times more likely than men to be admitted to the hospital for asthma.

Though these are the most apparent and referenced reasons for differences between male and female rates of asthma before and after puberty, there are other factors that may play into these trends. For example, women generally have smaller airways compared to men, which may contribute to increased airway resistance and susceptibility to asthma symptoms. Smaller airways can also make it more difficult to clear mucus and other irritants, leading to greater airway inflammation and obstruction. Women also tend to have stronger immune responses and which can lead to heightened inflammation and airway reactivity in response to asthma triggers. In order to ascertain these factors as contributing to the trends seen between men and women with asthma, more empirical research is needed.

Asthma Across Age in the USA

In addition to gender, we can also examine asthma prevalence by age.

Based on the analysis of current asthma prevalence in the United States by age group, we found that young adults (age groups 15-19 and 20-24) had the highest prevalences of asthma, with 9.3% and 10.3% respectively. This increase in prevalence for those from birth up to age 24 might be due to changes in how asthma is diagnosed and accounted for and better awareness of asthma and thus diagnoses systems for adults. Or, it could also be due to the fact that false attribution of other respiratory symptoms to doctor-diagnosed asthma might have occurred for adults.

An increase of asthma prevalence for those who have reached adulthood might also be due to the factor of adult-onset asthma. Previous studies have shown that adult-onset asthma is more non-allergenic and more prevalent in women (Rao & Mishra, 2023). Similarly, another factor that might influence the higher prevalence of asthma in higher age groups is occupational asthma or environmental risk factors. For example, different factors like long-term exposure to O3 air pollution, organic dusts, and cleaning agents have all been shown to be associated with asthma onset for adults (Le Moual et al., 2013). These factors may explain the relatively constant prevalence of asthma in the three oldest age groups as opposed to the two youngest age groups. In addition to those who received diagnoses of asthma in childhood, the higher age groups would also include those with occupational or environment-affected asthma development.

The graph depicting asthma mortality rates by age in the USA clearly shows that the elderly population has a significantly higher mortality rate from asthma compared to younger people. This trend can be attributed to several age-related factors. As people age, their immune systems typically weaken, and lung function naturally declines. This can make the elderly more vulnerable to respiratory conditions, including the severity of asthma attacks which can be fatal.

Furthermore, older adults may have a range of comorbidities, such as COPD or heart disease, which can complicate asthma management and exacerbate its symptoms. The ability to recover from asthma exacerbations also diminishes with age, increasing the risk of mortality. Moreover, polypharmacy, which refers to the use of multiple medications, is common in this age group and can lead to drug interactions that potentially worsen asthma control (Hanania & Sharafkhaneh, 2018).

Additionally, there may be social and behavioral factors at play. Older adults are often less likely to recognize or report symptoms of asthma. They might downplay their symptoms or attribute them to general aging, leading to under-treatment. Healthcare access issues, cognitive decline, or physical limitations can also interfere with effective asthma management in older adults. All these aspects combined contribute to the higher rates of asthma-related deaths in the elderly as depicted in the graph. For further details, the CDC Asthma Hub and the referenced studies provide a wealth of information on asthma management and risks in older adults (Baptist & Busse, 2014; AAFA, n.d.).

After examining the factors contributing to the higher mortality rates from asthma in the elderly population, it's important to shift our focus to another vulnerable group: infants. While the elderly face age-related declines in lung function and immune response, resulting in a higher mortality rate from asthma, they are less likely to experience frequent asthma attacks compared to younger age groups due to various factors such as decreased physical activity and exposure to environmental triggers. In contrast, infants, with their developing respiratory systems and susceptibility to respiratory viruses, are particularly prone to asthma attacks. Babies are more susceptible to asthma attacks due to several factors. Their smaller airways can easily become blocked or irritated by viral infections, tight airways, or mucus buildup, making breathing difficult. Additionally, infants may lack the ability to effectively communicate their symptoms, leading to delays in diagnosis and treatment. Respiratory viruses, common in children under 5, are a major trigger for asthma symptoms in infants, with at least half of asthmatic children showing signs before age 5 (Vogelberg, 2019). Moreover, infants with a family history of asthma or allergies, specific allergies, or exposure to maternal smoking during pregnancy are at higher risk of developing asthma early in life. Identifying asthma in infants can be challenging due to overlapping symptoms with other conditions like bronchiolitis, croup, or pneumonia. Diagnostic methods such as lung function tests are often impractical, and doctors may rely on observing the child's response to medications, blood tests, allergy testing, and X-rays for diagnosis. Treatment involves administering asthma medications tailored for infants, typically through nebulizers or inhalers with spacers and masks. Caregivers should develop an Asthma Action Plan with their child's doctor to manage symptoms effectively and seek emergency care when necessary.

Hospitalizations for asthma are most prevalent for infants, toddlers, and young children in the United States. This prevalence in pediatric hospitalization for asthma can potentially be explained by several factors, including differences in immune systems for infants and higher severity for younger populations, diagnostic issues in early childhood, and repeat hospitalizations. First, the immune systems of infants and children are much weaker than adults, making them more susceptible to respiratory infections such as those from RSV (respiratory syncytial virus) and rhinovirus. Research has shown that such viral infections are the most common cause of symptom exacerbation in children. Physiologically, infants and children have smaller airways which contribute to the increased severity of symptoms due to blocked airways (Luz Garcia-Garcia et al., 2016).

Another reason for higher rates of asthma in the youngest cohorts is the difficulty of asthma diagnosis for these age groups. First, asthma might be hard to diagnose for children and infants, who either cannot or have more difficulty communicating their symptoms. Secondly, due to asthma symptoms being very similar to other disease symptoms that young children might suffer severely from (e.g. pneumonia), the higher number of hospitalizations might also account for misdiagnosed cases. The diagnosis of asthma relies on direct methods like lung function tests, which might be difficult or risky to perform for infants. Thus, hospitalizations attributed to asthma might be overcounted for infants since their diagnoses might depend on less reliable methods (Asthma and Allergy Foundation of America, 2021).

Finally, repeat hospitalizations for infants and young children might also explain why case counts are highest for this age group despite mortality rates being highest for older adults. The data does not count unique patient cases or new hospitalizations, but rather total numbers of hospitalizations. Research has shown that readmissions for severe asthma are prevalent for the “highest-risk” children, with up to one in four youth being rehospitalized within just thirty days (Kenyon et al., 2019). Repeat hospitalizations might also be attributed to the fact that parents and caregivers of these age groups are more aware and likely to seek medical attention for their children at younger ages. This proactiveness might explain any repeat hospitalizations and/or assessments that contribute to higher counts of hospitalizations for pediatric asthma patients.

Asthma with Other Diseases in the USA

In medical terms, a comorbidity refers to the coexistence of two or more diseases or medical conditions in the same individual, where one is usually the primary condition and the others are additional. This concept helps in understanding how various diseases interact and affect an individual's overall health and treatment outcomes​.

In the context of asthma, obesity and COPD are common additional health problems. Obesity can worsen asthma symptoms due to increased inflammation and physical pressure on the lungs. COPD and asthma often occur together, which can make diagnosing and managing each condition more complicated. These links are crucial to consider in treating and understanding asthma effectively.

Looking at the relative concentrations of individuals with asthma, COPD, and obesity among all 50 states, there seems to be some level of correlation between the three, but mostly between COPD and obesity. For example, in the Southeast United States, there is a high concentration of both obese individuals and individuals diagnosed with COPD.

Asthma and COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease) are both chronic respiratory conditions with overlapping symptoms and risk factors, leading to a notable correlation between the two. This correlation is largely due to the inflammatory processes and the pathophysiological traits they share, which can manifest in individuals as Asthma-COPD Overlap (ACO). ACO is characterized by features typical of both diseases, such as airway hyperresponsiveness and obstruction, and tends to have a greater disease burden compared to each condition alone. Individuals with ACO often experience an earlier onset of symptoms and a steeper decline in lung function over time, necessitating a broader and more intense range of treatments (ERJ ERS Journals, 2022; BioMed Central, 2023).

On the other hand, the correlation between asthma and obesity is significant due to the mechanical and inflammatory impacts of excess body weight on lung function. Obesity can exacerbate asthma by reducing lung volume, altering airway responsiveness, and increasing inflammation, thereby intensifying asthma symptoms and control issues. The link between COPD and obesity is less direct and not as strong; while obesity can worsen breathlessness in COPD patients, it doesn’t have as direct a role in the development or exacerbation of COPD as it does with asthma (ERJ ERS Journals, 2022; BioMed Central, 2023).

Thus, the interconnections between asthma and COPD are more intrinsic due to the shared aspects of airway disease, while the relationship between asthma and obesity involves additional mechanisms related to body weight's impact on respiratory physiology. This explains why asthma is more closely linked with both COPD and obesity independently, rather than a direct correlation between COPD and obesity itself.

Our computed correlation matrix displays measures of geographic association between the afflictions of COPD, obesity, and asthma. Greater absolute values represent a stronger association, and values closer to 0 represent a lack of association. Interestingly, as mentioned previously, COPD and obesity show the strongest correlation coefficient, a measure that aims to gauge the strength of a linear relationship between two variables, ranging from -1 to 1. This may be due to the negative effects of obesity on respiratory disease, as buildup of fat around the heart, lungs, and chest, commonly associated with obesity, inhibit the functioning of those organs, which can exacerbate respiratory conditions. Additionally, about 65% of the COPD-having population falls in the overweight or obese categories (Verberne et al. 63), so geographic associations may be more pronounced between the two afflictions; in comparison, the asthmatic obesity rate was about 38.8% in the US in 2010. Though this figure is statistically larger than the nonasthmatic obesity rate (26.8%), it still is far less than the percentage of overweight or obese COPD-having individuals (CDC).

While asthma can also be influenced by environmental geographic factors, such as air pollution and external allergens, its geographical distribution may be less pronounced compared to COPD and obesity. This may be due to differences in the underlying mechanisms and triggers of asthma, such as genetic factors, allergies, and respiratory infections. Additionally, asthma may not always be solely influenced by external factors and can have a significant genetic component, which results in its geographical association being far less straightforward.

Asthma Triggers in the USA

Asthma is influenced by a variety of both indoor and outdoor factors. These include environmental factors such as smoking and cigarette usage, wildfires, allergens, and pollutants, all of which have long been associated with the disease. It is thus of interest to study the geographic distribution of environmental factors in comparison to that of the adult asthmatic population in the United States. Such analysis may reveal better understanding of asthma triggers and development in addition to potential public health strategies on a state-by-state level.

For example, it is worthwhile to study asthmagens, or known chemicals such as volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that cause asthma. Formaldehyde and acetaldehyde are two such VOCs that have been related to occupational asthma, so studying the environmental exposure to these chemicals would be key (Currie & Ayres, 2005). In addition, studying rates of asthma in relation to levels of smoking and cigarette usage is also essential because smoke, including secondhand tobacco smoke, has long been known as a trigger for asthma (Booalayan et al., 2020). Similarly, examining the relation of regions with frequent wildfires (and thus associated smoke) and rates of asthma might be helpful to understand such high-risk asthma areas and think of management efforts for air-quality related asthma prevalence.

Specifically, we chose the chemicals formaldehyde and acetaldehyde to examine the geographic distribution of. When organic materials such as vegetation and biomass are burned, various volatile organic compounds (VOCs) are released into the atmosphere, including formaldehyde. Formaldehyde was hence chosen to study, due to its status as one of the most abundant non-methane volatile organic compounds; as a byproduct of incomplete combustion of organic matter, wildfires can produce significant amounts of formaldehyde along with other pollutants. Additionally, formaldehyde can be present in various indoor products commonly found in homes and workplaces, including building materials such as plywood, particleboard, and insulation, as well as household items like composite wood furniture, cabinets, and flooring; formaldehyde is commonly used in the production of these materials as a resin or adhesive to provide strength, durability, and moisture resistance. Additionally, formaldehyde may be present in permanent press fabrics, personal care products, and certain cleaning agents, and combustion sources like tobacco smoke and gas appliances can also release formaldehyde indoors. Long-term exposure to elevated levels of formaldehyde has adverse health effects, including respiratory irritation, allergy triggering, and potential carcinogenic effects (Liao et al. 18319-18331).

There is not a huge similarity between the distribution of formaldehyde and asthma in the United States. In fact, California and some select states in the south have the highest percentage of formaldehyde in the atmosphere, but most of the states with the highest percentage of asthmatic adults are in the Northern United States, including Oregon and most of New England. A possible explanation for why formaldehyde could be darkened in California is because of the frequency of wildfires that occur at a much greater rate than most of the country. Meanwhile, a possible explanation for the increased rates of formaldehyde in the South could be due to more smokers being present in those areas. Formaldehyde emissions tend to increase in places with high humidity, and ozone also helps it exacerbate problems (Van Evra, 2019). A possible reason that the formaldehyde and asthma distributions across the country are not very similar could be that pollutants as a whole could cause asthma, but not specifically one chemical. Formaldehyde is also really harmful at high concentrations, which people may not be exposed to very often.

Another airborne substance that would trigger asthma is cigarette smoke. Smoking is most prevalent in the states of West Virginia, Kentucky, Louisiana, and New Jersey, while asthma is most prevalent in the Northern states, indicating a discrepancy in trends. However, West Virginia and Kentucky both have higher asthma levels than any state surrounding them, possibly indicating a correlation between smoking and asthma. While it is certain that tobacco smoke is a powerful asthma trigger, it may not be as large of a cause as anticipated. Instead, smoking exacerbates the negative symptoms of asthma, leading to more frequent and severe attacks, and causing medication to work less effectively (Cleveland Clinic, 2021). Not only does smoking affect asthma, but exposure to secondhand smoke is also detrimental and is connected with more severe symptoms. But, asthma can be caused by a variety of factors, which may interact and work with each other, and asthma being more prevalent in the North could be due to those other factors.

Additionally, acetaldehyde is another VOC commonly found in tobacco burning, as well as various industrial emissions. Acetaldehyde was chosen to study due to its status as one of the leading harmful chemicals produced during the combustion of tobacco in cigarettes, cigars, and other tobacco products, associated with various health risks, including cancer and respiratory diseases. Additionally, acetaldehyde is emitted from vehicle exhaust, industrial emissions, and combustion processes. Acetaldehyde can be emitted as a byproduct of industrial processes, particularly those involving combustion or chemical synthesis. Industries such as chemical manufacturing, petroleum refining, and metalworking may also acetaldehyde into the atmosphere as part of their emissions, contributing to air pollution and posing health risks to nearby communities.

Model

The ability to predict asthma prevalence accurately is crucial for effective healthcare planning and individual risk management. This model aims to leverage both environmental and genetic factors to estimate the likelihood of asthma in individuals based on several key parameters we explored, specifically US State (location), gender, cigarette usage, average annual income and family history of asthma. These inputs are utilized to provide tailored asthma risk assessments.

Using the data we collected for the above analysis, we prepared our data for our model by handling missing values and encoding categorical variables like 'State' and 'Gender'. States were one-hot encoded to create a binary column for each, while gender was simply converted to a binary variable (0 for Female and 1 for Male). Income data was converted to a binary variable as well, and income data was then normalized to level the playing field across different economic conditions, ensuring the fairness and accuracy of the model.

Ridge Regression was chosen for model building due to its effectiveness in managing multicollinearity, which is a common issue with one-hot encoded data and overlapping demographic variables. The model was trained on 80% of the dataset, and the remaining 20% was kept for validation. We used performance metrics such as Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and the coefficient of determination (R^2) to indicate high reliability and predictive capability. Our model demonstrated high accuracy, with a RMSE of 1.33 and a R^2 of 0.60, indicating that the model could explain 80% of the variance in asthma prevalence among the dataset.

We also adjusted the model to account for family history of asthma. If a family history of asthma is indicated, the prediction is adjusted upwards by a set percentage, in this case by 10%.

We then integrated the model into an interactive tool that enables users to input their personal data and receive an immediate asthma prevalence prediction. This tool is particularly useful for individuals seeking insights into their health risks.

Conclusions and Next Steps

Overall, asthma is associated with many factors, and the trends were explored in relation to gender, age, and possible exacerbating factors, including cigarette smoke, comorbidities, obesity, asthma triggers, and poverty. We were intrigued to find strong associations between some variables and weaker-than-expected ones for others, and then dove further into the potential reasoning behind these trends, in order to deepen our understanding of why asthma occurs and how it does to create a comprehensive asthmatic prevalence likelihood model.

Given additional time and resources to broaden our field of study, there are many possible avenues of research and visualization that would be quite worthwhile to investigate. One interesting branch to develop would revolve around investigation of specific genetic factors linked with asthma in young children. Additionally, ascertaining whether certain factors affect those pregnant with asthmatic children would be interesting, as would studying associations of asthma with other comorbidities, such as GERD or rhinitis. Furthermore, though there is no outright cure for asthma, we also could investigate whether certain methods of prevention or deterrence have a statistically significant effectiveness rate compared to others.

Another area of study would be investigating the impact of specific environmental pollutants on asthma incidence. While we did look into and saw very little correlation between AQI (the Air Quality Index) and asthma, it may be worthwhile to investigate how specific levels of particulate matter, ozone and nitrogen dioxide interact with asthma prevalence rates. Moreover, it would be interesting to explore the effects of lifestyle choices on asthma management, such as diet, exercise and stress levels.
